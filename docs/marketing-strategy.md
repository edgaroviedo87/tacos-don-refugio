# Tacos Don Refugio — Marketing & Growth Strategy
### "El Placer del Sabor" · Villa de Álvarez, Colima
**Document type:** Long-term strategic vision (Phase 1 website → Phase 4 mobile ordering app)
**Prepared for:** Ownership / "Equipo Don Refugio"
**Horizon:** 24–36 months · **Review cadence:** quarterly

---

## 0. Executive Summary

Tacos Don Refugio is not a startup trying to earn trust — it is a multi-decade local institution ("toda una tradición en Villa de Álvarez") with ~10k Facebook followers, three branches, and 90% recommendation. That existing equity is the single biggest asset and the cheapest growth lever available. The strategic job is **not** to build a brand from scratch; it is to **digitize an already-loved brand and capture the customer relationship before the marketplaces (Didi Food / Uber Eats) own it.**

The end goal — owner's vision — is a **direct online ordering channel and mobile app** that eliminates 25–30% marketplace commissions and gives the business its own customer data and CRM. The honest strategic truth: **an app is the last step, not the first.** Apps fail when there's no owned audience to put on them. So the entire roadmap is sequenced to build the **owned audience and ordering habit first** (website + WhatsApp + loyalty list), then layer technology on top of demand that already exists.

Four phases:
1. **Website + audience building** (now) — turn 10k passive social followers into an owned, contactable list and route delivery orders through WhatsApp.
2. **Online menu + admin** — single source of truth for menu/prices across branches; operational readiness.
3. **Direct online ordering + payments** — own the transaction, own the margin.
4. **Mobile app + CRM/push** — retention engine and loyalty at scale.

Guiding principle throughout: **regional small-business reality.** Family-run, budget-conscious, lean team. Every tactic below is chosen because it is cheap, durable, and runnable by the existing team — not because it looks good in a deck.

## 1. Market & Positioning

### 1.1 The geographic reality
Villa de Álvarez (municipality of Villa de Álvarez) is a regional hub city of ~100k+ people in southern Colima — large enough to sustain delivery and digital ordering, small enough that **word-of-mouth and local reputation dominate**. This is a market where "everyone knows everyone," where a brand with decades of history and 90% recommendation has a moat that no new competitor can buy. Digital is an amplifier of that reputation, not a replacement for it.

### 1.2 Customer segments
| Segment | Who | What they value | Primary channel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Families ("la familia con antojo")** | Parents ordering for the household, weekend meals | Portions, value, órdenes/combos, "comida que rinde" | Delivery / pickup, group orders |
| **Young locals (18–34)** | Students, young workers, the IG generation | Craving, speed, late-night, social proof, photogenic food | IG/WhatsApp, mobile ordering |
| **Workers / lunch crowd** | Daytime weekday demand near branches | Fast, reliable, fair price, close | Pickup, dine-in, "De Pasadita" |
| **Loyal regulars ("los de siempre")** | Multi-year repeat customers | Tradition, consistency, being recognized | All — these are the loyalty backbone |
| **Event / game-night occasion** | Group orders around fútbol, gatherings | Volume, combos, "el partido termina, el antojo comienza" | Delivery, pre-orders |

### 1.3 Dayparts & occasions
- **Lunch (comida, ~2–5pm):** weekday workers, families — the reliable base.
- **Night / antojo (8pm–late):** the emotional peak; tacos are a craving food. This is where "antojo" messaging and late ordering convert hardest.
- **Weekend family meals:** highest ticket (órdenes, combos for the household).
- **Event-driven spikes:** fútbol matches, paydays (quincena: 15th & 30th), local festivities (Fiestas de Octubre / patron-saint celebrations). These are predictable demand surges to plan promotions around.

### 1.4 Competitive context
Competition is **other local taquerías and street stands**, plus the marketplaces themselves (Didi/Uber Eats) which flatten every restaurant into an interchangeable list sorted by price and delivery time. The strategic risk of the marketplaces is not just commission — it's **commoditization**: a customer who orders "tacos" on Didi has no relationship with Don Refugio. Don Refugio's advantage over both is the same: **a real, decades-old brand with genuine local love.** The strategy is to make that advantage legible online.

### 1.5 Positioning statement
> **For the people of Villa de Álvarez who crave real tacos, Tacos Don Refugio is the taquería that has earned the city's trust for years — "El Placer del Sabor" — delivering the antojo of authentic, generous, made-with-pride tacos, now as easy to order as it is impossible to resist.**

**Why people choose Don Refugio (the three pillars of preference):**
1. **Tradition & trust** — "toda una tradición", a known name, consistency over years.
2. **The antojo** — flavor and generous portions that create genuine craving ("antojos que valen la pena", "buen sabor, buenas porciones").
3. **Local pride & warmth** — it's *ours*, family-run, "equipo Don Refugio," part of the city's identity.

The modernization must protect pillars 1 and 3 while making pillar 2 *frictionless to act on.*

## 2. Brand Strategy

### 2.1 The core tension to manage
Modernizing a beloved traditional brand has one failure mode: **looking so polished it stops feeling authentic.** The equity is *tradition + warmth.* Therefore the rule is: **modernize the convenience, not the soul.** Sleek ordering, slow-cooked identity. The brand should feel like the same taquería the city loves — just easier to reach.

### 2.2 Brand evolution path
- **Keep (non-negotiable equity):** the name, the years of tradition, the "equipo Don Refugio" human warmth, "El Placer del Sabor," local Villa de Álvarez identity, the antojo emotion.
- **Sharpen:** a consistent visual system (the existing palette is already strong — green #1E8A3C, orange-red #E8431F, gold #F2B705, cream #FBF7F0, dark brown #2B2118), consistent logo usage, and above all **consistently appetizing food photography.**
- **Add:** digital convenience, a unified menu, an owned customer relationship, and a loyalty layer — all framed as "we're making it easier to enjoy Don Refugio," never as "we changed."

### 2.3 Voice & tone
Warm, proud, playful, craving-centric, and unmistakably local. Talks like a person from Villa de Álvarez, not a corporate chain. Uses real local Spanish and the existing copy DNA. Confident but never arrogant; the brand has nothing to prove, it just invites you in.

### 2.4 Messaging pillars
Every piece of communication should ladder up to one of these four:

1. **El antojo (Craving)** — the emotional core. *"Antojos que valen la pena", "El partido termina, el antojo comienza."* Food-forward, sensory, photo-driven.
2. **Tradición & confianza (Tradition & trust)** — the equity. *"Toda una tradición en Villa de Álvarez", "Sabores que hablan por sí mismos."*
3. **Sabor & porción (Flavor & value)** — the rational reason to buy. *"Buen sabor, buenas porciones."*
4. **Cercanía & comunidad (Closeness & community)** — local pride, the team, the customers. *"Pide, disfruta y regresa", "equipo Don Refugio."*

**Tagline architecture:** keep "El Placer del Sabor" as the master brand line; rotate the existing copys as campaign/seasonal lines. Do not invent a new slogan — the existing ones already test well with the audience.

## 3. Digital Presence Strategy

### 3.1 Role of each channel (clear division of labor)
The most common small-business mistake is treating every channel the same. Each has one job:

| Channel | Primary job | Secondary job |
|---|---|---|
| **Website (owned)** | The **conversion + data-capture hub.** Menu, branches, hours, order CTA (WhatsApp now → ordering later), loyalty signup. The home base everything else points to. | Local SEO, credibility |
| **Google Business Profile (GBP)** | **Discovery.** "tacos cerca de mí" / "tacos Villa de Álvarez" — capturing high-intent local search. One profile per branch. | Reviews, hours, photos, directions |
| **Facebook (~10k)** | **Reach + community + announcements.** The biggest existing asset; broad local audience, families. | Drive to WhatsApp/website |
| **Instagram (@tacosdonrefugio)** | **Antojo engine.** Food photography, reels, younger audience, UGC. | Brand culture, behind-the-scenes |
| **WhatsApp** | **The ordering + relationship channel TODAY.** Where phone delivery moves to; the bridge to owned ordering before the app exists. | CRM-lite, broadcast lists |

**Strategic point:** social media is *rented land* — Meta owns the followers, the algorithm throttles reach. The website + WhatsApp list + loyalty database are *owned land.* The entire digital strategy is about **converting rented reach into owned relationships.**

### 3.2 Local SEO priorities (high ROI, low cost)
For a single-city business, local SEO is the highest-leverage free channel:
1. **Claim, verify, and fully optimize a Google Business Profile for each of the 3 branches** (Matriz Av. 1° de Mayo, Portal del Centro, De Pasadita) — correct name, address, hours, phone, category ("Taquería" / "Restaurante mexicano"), and the order/WhatsApp link.
2. **Photos on GBP** — appetizing food + storefront. GBP photos drive clicks and "antojo" more than text.
3. **Reviews engine** — actively ask happy customers for Google reviews (QR at the counter, link in WhatsApp after order). The current 15 reviews / 90% is good but thin; **getting to 100+ reviews per branch is a realistic, transformative 12-month goal.**
4. **Website NAP consistency** (Name, Address, Phone identical everywhere) + a page per branch + LocalBusiness schema markup.
5. Target queries: *"tacos Villa de Álvarez", "tacos a domicilio Villa de Álvarez", "taquería cerca de mí", "tortas Villa de Álvarez."*

### 3.3 Content engine — the "antojo" machine
Food photography **is** the marketing for a taquería. A bad photo of a great taco loses the sale; a great photo of a great taco closes it. Priorities:
- **Invest once in a proper food photo/video shoot** of the hero items (tacos, órdenes, mulitas, tortas, aguas frescas). This is the single best small-budget marketing investment available and feeds website, GBP, IG, and FB for a year.
- **Sustainable weekly cadence the team can actually keep** (don't over-promise content):
  - 2–3 IG/FB posts + 1–2 reels/week.
  - Content mix: 50% antojo (food close-ups, "sizzle"), 20% behind-the-scenes / equipo Don Refugio (humanizes the brand), 20% UGC/reviews/customer love, 10% promo/announcements.
- **UGC flywheel:** encourage customers to tag @tacosdonrefugio; repost the best. Run a simple "etiquétanos para salir en nuestras historias" mechanic. Free, authentic, and builds community.
- **Tie content to the calendar:** game nights, quincena, weekends, local festivities — pre-plan posts around predictable demand.

## 4. The Owned-Ordering Thesis (the heart of the vision)

### 4.1 The business case in plain numbers
Marketplace commissions in Mexico (Didi Food / Uber Eats) typically run **~25–30% of order value**, sometimes more once "marketing/visibility" fees are added. For a low-margin food business, that is often **the entire profit margin on a delivery order.**

Illustrative economics on a $300 MXN order (numbers to validate with the owner's real costs):

| | Via marketplace (~28%) | Via owned channel |
|---|---|---|
| Order value | $300 | $300 |
| Commission | −$84 | $0 |
| Own delivery cost (est.) | $0 (included) | −$30 to −$45 |
| Payment processing | included | ~−$10 (3–3.5%) |
| **Net retained** | **~$216** | **~$245–260** |
| **Customer data captured** | **None** | **Full** |

Even after paying for your own delivery and payment processing, owned ordering **retains meaningfully more margin per order** — and the second, larger prize is **the customer data**: who they are, what they order, how often. The marketplace keeps that data and rents your own customers back to you.

### 4.2 Why direct wins (beyond margin)
1. **Margin** — keep the 25–30% instead of donating it.
2. **Data & CRM** — order history enables loyalty, push, win-back, and demand forecasting. *This is the asset the app is really built to exploit.*
3. **Relationship** — direct contact (WhatsApp, push) means you can reactivate a customer for free forever, instead of paying for visibility each time.
4. **Brand control** — your menu, your photos, your experience, not a commoditized list.

### 4.3 The migration risks (be honest)
- **Marketplaces provide demand and logistics.** Cutting them entirely on day one is a mistake. The right move is **coexistence then migration**, not a cold switch.
- **Delivery logistics** become *your* problem on direct orders (own drivers, or a "pickup-first" strategy to start).
- **Customer habit** — people default to the app they already have. You must give them a *reason* to order direct (loyalty, exclusive deals, "order direct and save / get points").
- **Operational load** on a family team — adding order channels without process will create chaos. Sequencing matters.

**Recommended posture:** Keep marketplaces as a customer-acquisition funnel, but treat every marketplace order as a *lead to convert to direct.* Insert a small printed card / sticker in marketplace orders: *"La próxima, pide directo por WhatsApp y gana premios — sin esperas, mejor precio."* Convert their customers into your customers.

### 4.4 What Phase 1 (the website) must do RIGHT NOW to build the owned audience
Even before any ordering tech exists, Phase 1 must start accumulating owned assets:
1. **WhatsApp ordering as the primary CTA** — "Pide por WhatsApp" buttons everywhere (site, GBP, IG bio, FB). This replaces phone-only delivery and starts building a **contact list** automatically.
2. **Data capture** — a simple loyalty/VIP signup ("Únete al Club Don Refugio — promos y premios"): name, phone/WhatsApp, birthday, favorite branch. Even a basic form into a spreadsheet/Sheets is enough to start. **Every order and signup is a future app user.**
3. **WhatsApp Broadcast lists / WhatsApp Business** — start segmenting customers now so that when promos run, there's an owned audience to message for free.
4. **Reviews & social proof capture** — funnel happy customers to Google reviews.
5. **Branch + menu + hours** clearly presented so the website becomes the canonical "where/what/how to order."

The strategic insight: **the contact list you build in Phase 1 is the launch audience for the app in Phase 4.** Start it now.

## 5. Customer Retention & Loyalty

Acquisition in a small city saturates fast — there are only so many people. **Retention and frequency are where the real growth is.** Getting an existing customer to go from 2x/month to 3x/month is cheaper and bigger than finding new customers.

### 5.1 Loyalty program ideas suited to a taquería
- **"Club Don Refugio" points / digital stamp card** — e.g., earn points per order, or "compra 9 órdenes, la 10ª va por cuenta de la casa." Taco/torta businesses are perfect for frequency mechanics. Start manual (WhatsApp + spreadsheet or a cheap digital stamp tool), formalize in the app later.
- **Frequency rewards** — reward the *regulars* ("los de siempre") who are already the backbone; recognition matters as much as discounts in a warm local brand.
- **Family / órden deals** — combos that increase ticket size and serve the family segment ("Combo Familiar Don Refugio"). Bundle tacos + aguas + órdenes.
- **Birthday reward** — capture birthday at signup → free taco/agua on their birthday. Cheap, emotional, drives data capture.
- **"Pide directo" incentive** — the migration tool: direct orders earn double points or a small perk vs. marketplace. This is how you actively pull customers off Didi/Uber.
- **Referral** — "trae a un amigo / recomiéndanos" — natural in a word-of-mouth town.
- **Occasion campaigns** — quincena promos, game-night combos, festival specials.

### 5.2 How the future app supercharges this
Once the app exists (Phase 4), loyalty stops being manual:
- **Automatic points & tiers** tied to real order history.
- **Push notifications** — the free, owned re-engagement channel ("Es viernes, ¿ya pediste tu antojo?", "Te extrañamos — 2x1 hoy"). Push is the app's killer feature for a craving-driven food brand.
- **Personalized offers** — "tu pedido de siempre" one-tap reorder; win-back for lapsed customers.
- **CRM segmentation** — VIPs, lapsed, high-value families, late-night crowd — each messaged differently.
- **Demand data** — know what sells when, by branch, to plan inventory and staffing.

The app's real product is not "ordering" (the website will already do that) — it's **retention, reorder convenience, and push-driven frequency.** That's the justification for building it.

## 6. Phased Go-To-Market Roadmap

> Each phase ties marketing to product capability. KPIs are deliberately few and trackable by a small team.

### Phase 1 — Website + Audience Building *(now — Months 0–3)*
**Marketing objective:** Convert existing social reach and phone customers into an *owned, contactable audience*; establish digital home base and local search dominance.

**Key tactics:**
- Launch website: menu, 3 branches, hours, "Pide por WhatsApp" CTA, "Club Don Refugio" signup.
- Claim + optimize all 3 Google Business Profiles; launch review drive.
- Professional food photo/video shoot (hero items).
- Set up WhatsApp Business + broadcast lists; route delivery to WhatsApp.
- Announce the "new, easier Don Refugio" across FB/IG (without losing tradition framing).

**KPIs:**
- # of owned contacts captured (WhatsApp list + Club Don Refugio signups) — *the north-star of this phase.*
- Google reviews count & rating per branch (target trajectory toward 100+/branch).
- WhatsApp orders/week (proving the channel works).

---

### Phase 2 — Online Menu + Admin *(Months 3–6)*
**Marketing objective:** Operational readiness and a single source of truth; deepen the owned audience; prove menu/pricing consistency across branches.

**Key tactics:**
- Admin-managed digital menu (live prices, availability) — solves the "precios variables" problem with one place to update.
- Begin structured content calendar tied to dayparts/events.
- Formalize loyalty mechanics (digital stamp/points, even if semi-manual).
- Start converting marketplace customers to direct (insert cards in Didi/Uber orders).

**KPIs:**
- Owned audience growth rate (m/m).
- Repeat-order rate among Club Don Refugio members vs. non-members.
- Menu/price consistency across branches (operational KPI: zero discrepancies).

---

### Phase 3 — Direct Online Ordering + Payments *(Months 6–12)*
**Marketing objective:** Own the transaction and the margin; shift order volume from marketplaces and WhatsApp-manual to direct web ordering.

**Key tactics:**
- Launch web ordering with online payment (and cash option) + pickup-first, then delivery.
- "Pide directo y gana" campaign — actively incentivize direct over marketplace.
- Decide delivery model (own driver vs. on-demand courier vs. pickup focus).
- Heavy promotion of direct ordering to the owned list built in Phases 1–2.

**KPIs:**
- % of total delivery/pickup orders coming through *owned* channel (vs. marketplace).
- Commission $ saved (margin retained vs. marketplace baseline).
- Average order value & direct-order conversion rate.

---

### Phase 4 — Mobile App + CRM/Push *(Months 12–24+)*
**Marketing objective:** Maximize retention & frequency; make Don Refugio the default app on the regular's phone; reduce marketplace dependence to near-zero.

**Key tactics:**
- Launch app **to the owned audience built across Phases 1–3** (warm launch, not cold).
- Full loyalty/points/tiers + one-tap reorder.
- Push-notification lifecycle program (craving prompts, win-back, VIP, occasions).
- App-exclusive perks to drive adoption; sunset reliance on marketplaces for repeat customers.

**KPIs:**
- App MAU / monthly active orderers.
- Order frequency per customer (the retention north-star).
- % revenue from owned channels (target: majority); marketplace dependency trending down.

## 8. Risks & Assumptions to Validate with the Owner

**Assumptions to confirm:**
1. **Unit economics** — real food cost, current margin per order, and actual marketplace commission rate (to make the owned-ordering case with true numbers).
2. **Delivery capability** — can the family/team handle own delivery, or is pickup-first the realistic Phase 3 start? Is there budget for drivers/couriers?
3. **Team capacity** — who manages WhatsApp orders, content, and reviews day-to-day? Marketing dies without an owner. Realistic time budget matters more than ambition.
4. **Tech budget & comfort** — willingness to invest in website/ordering/app, and the team's comfort managing digital tools.
5. **Brand assets** — current logo files, photography rights, consistent branch info (NAP) for all 3 locations.

**Risks to manage:**
- **Over-automating too fast** — adding order channels faster than the kitchen/team can absorb creates a bad experience that damages the very reputation that is the brand's moat. Sequence deliberately.
- **Marketplace cold-turkey** — cutting Didi/Uber before owned demand exists would lose volume. Coexist, then migrate.
- **Authenticity erosion** — modernizing into something that feels corporate and loses the warmth. Guard the soul.
- **Content sustainability** — committing to a posting cadence the team can't keep; better to do less, consistently.
- **App built too early** — the biggest strategic risk: building the app before there's an owned audience and ordering habit to put on it. The app must be the *culmination* of audience-building, not the start.
- **Single-city ceiling** — Villa de Álvarez has finite population; long-term growth beyond saturation = frequency/retention first, and only later possible expansion to nearby towns.

### Closing strategic note
Tacos Don Refugio already has what most brands spend millions trying to buy: **genuine local love and trust built over years.** The mission of this entire program is simple — **don't let the marketplaces rent that love back to you.** Capture the relationship, build the owned audience now, and let each phase earn the right to the next. The app is the destination; the contact list you start building this week is the road that gets you there.
